The Invisible Bruise
---------------------
Aarav was a cheerful 13-year-old who loved drawing superheroes. Every evening after school, he posted his artwork on a social media app. His classmates often liked his drawings, and each notification made him smile.

One day, he uploaded a picture of a superhero protecting a city. Within minutes, a comment appeared:

*"Did a five-year-old draw this?"*

Then another:

*"This is so bad. Stop posting."*

Soon, fake accounts started sharing his drawings with laughing emojis. Some students from school joined in, posting edited versions of his artwork and making fun of him.

At first, Aarav ignored the comments.

"It's just a joke," he told himself.

But every day the messages became worse.

*"Nobody likes you."*

*"Delete your account."*

*"You're a loser."*

He stopped posting his drawings. Then he stopped talking to his friends. Even in class, he couldn't concentrate. The words on the screen followed him everywhere.

One evening, his younger sister noticed he hadn't picked up his sketchbook in days.

"What happened to the superhero artist?" she asked.

Aarav shrugged. "People online keep making fun of me."

Instead of laughing or saying "ignore them," she sat beside him and said, "Let's tell Mom."

Together, they explained everything.

Their mother reminded Aarav that cyberbullies often hide behind screens because they think they won't face consequences.

The next day, they made a plan.

First, Aarav **did not reply** to the hurtful messages. He realized that arguing with bullies often gives them the attention they want.

Next, he **took screenshots** of the comments and fake accounts as evidence.

Then, he **blocked** every account that bullied him.

After that, he **reported** the abusive accounts to the social media platform.

Since some of the bullies were classmates, Aarav and his parents also informed the school. The teachers spoke to the students involved and reminded everyone about responsible online behavior and its consequences.

A few days later, something unexpected happened.

One of Aarav's classmates, Maya, created a post saying,

*"Let's support creativity instead of tearing people down."*

Many students shared their own drawings and encouraged each other. Slowly, the negative comments disappeared.

Aarav gathered the courage to post another superhero drawing.

This time, the superhero wasn't fighting monsters.

He was holding a shield with one word written on it:

**Respect.**

The drawing received hundreds of supportive comments.

Aarav smiled again—not because everyone liked his art, but because he had learned something far more important.

Real strength isn't staying silent when someone hurts you.

Real strength is asking for help, protecting yourself, and standing up for kindness.

## Moral of the Story

Cyberbullying can hurt just as much as bullying in real life. If you experience it:

* Don't reply to bullies.
* Save evidence by taking screenshots.
* Block and report abusive accounts.
* Tell a trusted adult, parent, teacher, or guardian.
* Remember that the bully's words do not define your worth.
* Be kind online and support others who may be facing cyberbullying.

**Kindness is contagious. One supportive message can be stronger than a hundred hurtful ones.**
